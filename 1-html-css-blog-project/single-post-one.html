What Moves God: Need or Faith?

Author: Fayiah S. Tamba

One of my favorite American writers, Philip Yancey raised a contemporary theological issue admitting to complex suffering in a sinful and dying world and how God’s silence to a seemingly discouraged and disappointed Christians is a commonly asked question. He posed the question, “Where Is God When It Hurts?” (2010) to examine why God seems to be silent. Yancey interrogated the level of God's intervention in people's situations. A similar question could be posed today because of COVID-19. Sorrows, excruciating pains, and fears are ever-increasing in the world due to the spread and unbearable effects of COVID-19. Many reputable, lovely, and potential people had died because of this deadly pandemic. Both old and young, black and white races are being affected. Yet, the number of death is escalating daily. Unfortunately, no country seems to be exempt, leaving economies to crumble, families to starve, schools and universities to shut down, unemployment rate to escalate, as well as creating constant restrictions in religious worship centers among others. An attempt to rephrase Philip Yancey’s question is a needed challenge to inquire about what moves God to answer prayer – need or faith? An African writer presents an argument that I support and think complements Yancey’s question.

Thompson J. Washington in, The Faith Factor, argues that "God will not meet you at the point of your needs but the point of your faith." Few African Christians tend to believe the first statement. They consider Philippians 4:19, "But my God shall supply all your need according to his riches in glory by Christ Jesus." In this verse, Paul did not indicate the condition by which believers could access the riches of God, except for assuring that God possesses heavenly riches and is willing to enrich his people. On the contrary, those who believe that God moves based on faith tend to agree with Hebrews 11:6, “But without faith, it is impossible to please him: for he that cometh to God must believe that he is and that he is a rewarded of them that diligently seek him." Washington’s argument which emphasizes faith as a means by which God responds to us can be justified by citing God’s miraculous moves in human history. A classic example is the story of Abram, whose name was changed to Abraham (Genesis 15:6).

In Genesis 15, Abraham needed a son to continue his lineage and subsequently fulfill the promise of becoming the father of many nations. God recognized his need but could only act based on the level of Abraham's faith. When Abraham's faith in God was accounted as righteousness (v.6), all of his needs were addressed. Amid this COVID-19 global pandemic, Christians must continue to pray fervently to God through faith so he would meet them at the point of their needs. Paul reminds the Christians, "For we walk by faith, not by sight" (2 Corinthians 5:7). Regardless of the situation confronting the lives of Christians, faith permits God to act. Even if Christians should cry out to God, it must be done in faith, not as if they are complaining about their situation. If God had heard of the Israelites' cry and acted immediately, I suppose they must have expressed some level of faith (Exodus 3:7). God knows all of our needs but he does not respond to our needs. He responds to our faith. Throughout the ministry of Jesus, faith was a requirement for salvation, healing, deliverance, and miracles. All of those who came to Jesus and testified had faith.

Faith is the opposite of fear. Fear creates doubts and unbelief. Instead of being afraid of the effects of Coronavirus, Christians should trust in the power of God for healing, and acknowledge Jesus as the master healer. In March 2020, Liberia's first Coronavirus case was recorded: Nathaniel Blama was the first affected COVID-19 patient. Following the second week in April, James Reynold was diagnosed positive of Coronavirus. These two government officials, being Christians, trusted, and hoped in God for recovery. Mr. Blama a Baptist deacon and Mr. Reynold a Minister of the Gospel became a focal point for intercession. Prayers were made on their behalf and they survived the severity of Coronavirus (Acts 12:5). Reflecting on these testimonies, I recognize the faith of those prayerful and faithful intercessors. Therefore, those Christians who are discouraged and disappointed with God must understand that faith is a binding human response to get God's attention and that it leads to hope. Even if everything seems to be lost, all is not lost, because faith in God can change a story incredibly!
